Kinetics VIII#

This lecture introduces catalytic reactions. We develop strategies for the analysis of mechanisms catalyzed by enzymes.

Catalytic Reactions#

There are many cases where reactions of interest will not occur at appreciable rates at temperatures and/or pressures that are industrially feasible. Classically, for non-catalytic reactions, you really only have two handles for increasing reaction rate: increasing temperature and increasing concentration. There are practical limits to doing so. These include materials of construction (they may not be able to withstand very high temperatures or pressures); capital cost (it is expensive to construct reactors that can withstand high temperatures and pressures); cost of energy (you generally have to pay in energy costs to run reactions at high temperatures); cost of ancillary equipment (adding a high pressure pump or compressor is expensive; and selectivity reasons (generally, as temperatures increase, more reactions become kinetically accessible, and you lose your ability to control selectivity). More generally: we know that the cost of a process scales inversely with the rate at which that process occurs. Slow reactions require long batch times or very large flow reactors, so we are frequently interested in running reactions at maximum rate without incurring the difficulties associated with trying to increase reaction rates thermally (by heating). This is where catalysts come in.

Our basic idea of a chemical reaction is that it goes from a rectant state to a product state along some reaction coordinate. The energetic change between reactant state and product state is our energy change of reaction (this applies for any thermodynamic state function: enthalpy, entropy, free energy are most useful in reactions and reactor design). These quantities determine the equilibrium constant for the reaction, which tells us how far the reaction can go (i.e., it determines the system composition at chemical equilibrium). We also expect that as the reactant transforms into the product along that reaction coordinate, it will go through a transition state, which is usually higher in energy than both the reactant state and the product state. The energy change associated with forming the transition state is called an “activation barrier,” and we can think of enthalpies of activation \((\Delta H^\ddagger)\), entropies of activation \((\Delta S^\ddagger)\), and Gibbs Free Energies of activation \((\Delta G^\ddagger)\). These quantities determine the rate constant for the reaction, which then determines how fast the reaction occurs. In order to increase the rate of the reaction, it is desireable to reduce the free energy barrier associated with forming the transition state.

Generally speaking, a catalyst functions by providing an alternate path between the reactant state and the product state. It does this by coordinating to (loosely, binding with) reactants and products. This induces new types of strain or changes the electron density in key functional groups, which makes it easier to break or to form chemical bonds. One important thing to note is that the catalyst does not affect the reactant state or the product state. These are species present in a bulk gas or liquid phase. For example, whether or not we use a catalyst, the \(N_2\), \(H_2\), and \(N\!H_3\) species present in Ammonia synthesis are the same. Since enthalpies, entropies, and Gibbs free energies are thermodynamic state functions, this means that a catalyst does not affect the overall energetics of the macroscopic reaction that we observe in the laboratory. If a reaction is thermodyanmically unfavorable at a certain set of reaction conditions, a catalyst cannot change this.

A catalyst does provide a different path between the reactant state and the product state. Ideally, this new path will proceed through a lower energy transition state that is easier to form. The energetics of forming the transition state determine the rate constant for the reaction, so a catalyst will typically increase the rate of the reaction by virtue of allowing that reaction to proceed through a transition state that is lower in energy and thus easier to form. In other words, a catalyst does not change the equilibrium constant for an overall reaction, and it does not make the overall reaction any more or less favorable. A catalyst will increase the rate of reaction, so it increases how quickly that system gets to equilibrium.

Important

In simple terms, a catalyst will not change how far a reaction will go. A catalyst will only change how fast the reaction gets where it is going.

Mechanisms of Catalytic Reactions#

The above picture regarding a catalyst decreasing a barrier is conceptually useful; however, just like all reactions, catalytic reactions occur through a series of elementary steps, each with their own characteristic activation barriers (rate constants) and energy changes of reaction (equilibrium constants). These elementary steps will be different than those in the non-catalytic pathway. If we have designed a good catalyst, the net result is that the catalytic mechanism is more kinetically favorable than the non-catalytic mechanism. But it is important to remember that reactions almost always occur through a series of elementary steps, not as a single, overall step that converts reactants into products.

A more complete picture#

Most of us have a basic understanding of catalysis, we’ve probably heard that a catalyst accelerates a reaction but is not itself changed by a reaction. This is true at the macroscopic, observable scale that we work on in the laboratory. A more correct description is that, in the course of the catalytic mechanism, the catalyst is changed substantially as it interacts with the reactant and facilitates its conversion into the product. However, at the conclusion of the catalytic cycle, the product of the reaction disengages from the catalyst, and the catalyst is restored to its original form so that it can then interact with another reactant molecule and start the cycle over.

Types of Catalytic Reactions#

We will consider a few type of catalytic reactions:

  1. Homogeneous catalysis: In these examples, the catalyst, reactant, product, and all intermediates and transition states are present in a single phase.

  2. Heterogeneous catalysis: In these examples, at least one of the catalyst, reactant, product, intermediates, and/or transition states exist in a second phase.

  3. Biocatalysis: These reactions use enzymes to facilitate a reaction. They can be either homogeneous (enzyme in solution) or heterogeneous (enzyme immobilized on a support).

We will start with Biocatalysis because it follows naturally from our consideration of non-catalytic reactions using a Pseudo-Steady State Approximation.

Enzyme-catalyzed Reactions#

Enzymes are biological molecules comprised of proteins/amino acid and various co-factors, such as metals or metal ions, that are coordinated to the protein. They function as catalysts and are known for their specificity. They are extremely selective in their binding and the types of reactions they will facilitate. Basically, their structure/composition have been optimized over millinea by evolutionary processes, so they tend to be very efficient at catalyzing (accelerating) a specific process.

On the positive side, enzymes operate at low temperatures, they are usually very efficient, and they are extremely selective.

On the negative side, they are biological molecules (proteins) whose function depends on the specific way they are folded. Usually that optimal configuration is only attained in physiological conditions; for example, at approximately \(30^\circ C\), near neutral pH, and in aqueous solution. They are extremely sensitive to their environment, so changes in temperature, pH, or the presence of high concentrations of organic molecules will usually cause enzymes to denature and lose their catalytic function. For this reason they tend to work very well but only within a limited window of operating conditions. This constrains our ability to tune enzyme performance to match the demands of specific systems. Usually, enzyme catalyzed processes will be restricted to:

  1. Aqueous media

  2. \(pH = 4 - 9\)

  3. \(T = 27^\circ C\) to \(70^\circ C\)

That said, for reactions involving delicate and reactive substrates (like sugars and other biological molecules), enzymes can be very effective, and they are used in many relevant industrial processes. One of the most prominent examples of an enzyme catalyzed process is the production of high fructose corn syrup from starch using amylase and xylose isomerase. Amylase will selectively hydrolyse starches into glucose monomers, and xylose isomerase will isomerize glucose into fructose, hence producing “high fructose corn syrup” (note that corn has almost no fructose in it inherently).

Tip

You can recognize an enzyme by the suffix “ase” at the end of its name.

Models of Enzyme Binding#

The first step in an enzyme catalyzed reaction is that the enzyme \((E)\) will bind to the substrate \((S)\). There are two classic models of enzyme binding:

  1. Lock and Key Model

  2. Induced Fit Model

The latter is more generally accepted because enzymes, being large, flexible molecules that respond to their environment, are much more likely to alter their local structure slightly in response to the chemical functionality of a specific substrate than they are to have a rigid pocket that is perfectly matched to the substrate. Regardless, the basic idea is that the enzyme will bind with the substrate. This will then initiate a set of elementary reactions that convert the bound substrate into the product, which ultimately disengages from the enzyme. As such, the Enzyme is returned to its original state at the completion of the catalytic cycle, so we say that it facilitates the conversion of substrate into product without itself being consumed by the reaction.

Michaelis-Menten Mechanism#

The most basic mechanism for Enzyme catalysis is the Michaelis-Menten model. Conceptually, it says that the enzyme binds the substrate, converting it into a reactive enzyme-substrate complex. This intial binding step is assumed to be reversible. This enzyme substrate complex is then converted into the reaction product. In a baseline Michaelis-Menten model, we assume that the product does not bind with the enzyme and is released immediately upon its formation to irreversibly form the reaction product and a free enzyme. We can write this as an overall reaction and as a set of elementary steps:

Overall

\[E + S \longrightarrow E + P\]

Mechanism

(1)#\[\begin{align} E + S &\rightleftharpoons ES \\ ES &\rightarrow E + P \\ \end{align}\]

Important

If we add these two elementary steps, it gives us the overall reaction stoichiometry. This is a fundamental requirement – if our reaction mechanism cannot reproduce the overall stoichiometry, then it is incorrect or incomplete in some way.

Analysis of the Michaelis-Menten Mechanism#

Looking at the overall reaction stoichiometry, we see that the overall rate of reaction is equal to the rate of consumption of substrate \((S)\), and it is equal to the rate of formation of the product \((P)\). We will choose to write an overall rate expression based on the rate of product formation, which is usually the most convenient choice.

\[r = R_P\]

Now we look at the elementary steps; we can evaluate the production rate of \(P\) as usual. It only appears as a product in step 2, so \(R_P = r_2\), which means:

\[r = r_2\]

Now we expand the rate expression for step two:

\[r = k_2[ES]\]

This tells us that the rate of reaction has a first order dependence on the concentration of the enzyme-substrate complex, \([ES]\). Unfortunately, this isn’t particularly useful because we will have a difficult time measuring, quantifying, predicting, controlling, etc. the concentration of the enzyme substrate complex. Usually, the enzyme is present in trace amounts relative to the substrate, so we can approximately treat the enzyme-substrate complex as a “reactive intermediate” and apply the Pseudo-Steady State Hypothesis. As a reminder, the PSSH implies that the net production rate of the reactive intermediate is zero:

\[R_{ES} = 0 = r_1 - r_2\]

We can then expand the rate expressions:

\[0 = k_1[S][E] - k_{-1}[ES] - k_2[ES]\]

Our goal, as usual, is to write the concentration of the enzyme-substrate complex, \([ES]\), in terms of bulk species concentrations, like the substrate concentration, \([S]\). Technically, we will have trouble quantifying the free enzyme concentration [E] since it is present in trace quantities, and it is unclear how much of it is free [E] and how much is bound with the substrate [ES]. That said, for now, we can treat it as a “known” quantity for the purposes of algebraic manipulations because it will make our work cleaner, neater, and easier. Later in the solution, we will consider a strategy for quantifying the free enzyme concentration in terms of things that we can measure. With this in mind, the above PSSH on the enzyme-substrate complex results in one equation that is expressed in terms of one unknown, [ES]. The rest of the terms are rate constants, substrate concentration [S], and free enzyme concentration [E]. As far as our symbolic algebra is concerned, we can treat these as known quantities. We may then solve the above PSSH equation for [ES]:

\[[ES] = \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2} [S][E]\]

This result gives us the concentration of the catalyst-bound intermediate [ES] in terms of the free active sites [E]. When developing overall rate expressions from a catalytic reaction mechanism, the best practice is to always try to get all of the intermediates in this form before we deal with the free active sites. In this case, we only have one catalyst-bound intermediate, \(ES\). To express its concentration in terms of only bulk species, known quantities, and kinetic parameters, we have to resolve the concentration of free enzyme, \(E\). Any time we want to determine the concentration of free active sites in terms of concentrations of bulk species, we will do so using a site balance. A site balance basically says that the total amount of catalyst (enzyme) in the system remains constant. We may not be able to easily and separately quantify the free enzyme concentration, \([E]\) and the enzyme substrate complex concentration, \([ES]\), but we always know the total quantity of catalyst. In the special case of a constant density system or a constant volume batch reactor, we can conclude:

\[[E_t] = [E] + [ES]\]

In other words, the sum of free enzyme and enzyme substrate concentrations have to always be equal to the total enzyme concentration. This is useful because we control the total concentration of the enzyme that we add to the reactor, so this total quantity is always known to us. Because we will always use a site balance when analyzing catalytic reaction mechanisms, it is convenient to always express concentrations of catalyst-bound species as a (linear) functions of free sites concentration. The reason that it is convenient to do so is that we can then substitute the concentration of the catalyst bound intermediate into the site balance, and we will obtain the result that each term on the right hand side is a linear function of free site concentration:

\[[E_t] = [E] + \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2} [S][E]\]

Now, we can see that we only have a single “unknown” here, which is the free enzyme concentration. We can solve for [E] to get:

\[[E] = \frac{[E_t]}{1 + \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2}[S]}\]

And with that, we can begin making substitutions into our rate expression:

The starting point:

\[r = k_2[ES]\]

Next:

\[r = \frac{k_1k_2}{k_{-1} + k_2} [E][S]\]

Finally substituting in the free enzyme concentration:

\[r = \frac{k_1k_2[E_t][S]}{\Big(k_{-1} + k_2\Big)\left(1 + \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2}[S]\right)}\]

This is a correct expression, but it is a bit cumbersome, so we’ll do some factoring, cancelling, and lumping parameters. You will always process Enzyme Kinetics problems this way in this course. First, factoring the term before \([S]\) in the denominator:

\[r = \frac{k_1k_2[E_t][S]}{\frac{k_1(k_{-1} + k_2)}{k_{-1} + k_2}\left(\frac{k_{-1} + k_2}{k_1} + [S]\right)}\]

This gives us a few cancellations, resulting in a simplified expression:

\[r = \frac{k_2[E_t][S]}{\frac{k_{-1} + k_2}{k_1} + [S]}\]

As a final step, we will define two lumped parameters based on groups of terms that are in the above equation:

\[V_{\mathrm{max}} = k_2[E_t]\]

and

\[K_M = \frac{k_{-1} + k_2}{k_1}\]

This gives the classic Michaelis-Menten Rate Expression:

\[r = \frac{V_{\mathrm{max}}[S]}{K_M + [S]}\]

Important

In enzyme kinetics, the quantities \(V_{\mathrm{max}}\) and \(K_M\) always have the definitions presented above.

Analysis of the MM Rate Expression#

There are some important behaviors we can predict by considering the limiting values of the Michaelis-Mention rate expression at low and high substrate concentrations:

\[r = \frac{V_{\textrm{max}}[S]}{K_M + [S]}\]

If we consider the rate at low concentrations of substrate \(([S] \rightarrow 0)\), we conclude that, under these conditions, \(K_M >> [S]\), so the denominator term is \(\approx K_M\). This gives the following rate expression, which will applies at low substrate concentrations:

\[r = \frac{V_{\textrm{max}}}{K_M}[S]\]

Important

At low substrate concentrations, the rate of the enzymatic reaction predicted by a Michaelis-Menten mechanism will be first order in substrate concentration.

Now if we consider the limit at high substrate concentrations, i.e., \([S] \rightarrow \infty\), we conclude that, under these conditions, \(K_M << [S]\). In this case, our denominator is \(\approx [S]\), and we find the following rate expression applies:

\[r = \frac{V_{\textrm{max}}[S]}{[S]} = V_{\textrm{max}}\]

So at high concentrations of substrate, the reaction rate becomes zero order in substrate concentration, and the rate of reaction goes to “\(V_\textrm{max}\).” This is the origin of the term, \(V_{\mathrm{max}}\), as it refers to the maximum rate of reaction (sometimes called a reaction “velocity”) that can be achieved for a given total enzyme concentration. This transition from zero order to first order means that, beyond a certain point, increasing substrate concentration will not increase the rate of reaction further. Below, I illustrate the conceptual behavior of a MM rate law with some arbitrary values for \(K_M\) and \(V_\textrm{max}\).

Important

At high substrate concentrations, the rate of reaction predicted by a Michaelis-Menten mechanism will become independent of substrate concentration.

#######################################################################################
# Assign arbitrary values for KM and Vmax, the MM parameters                          #
#######################################################################################

KM   = 1 #mmol/L
Vmax = 1 #mmol/L/s

#######################################################################################
# Define a numpy array of substrate concentrations from 0 to 50 mmol/L                #
#######################################################################################

S    = np.linspace(0, 50, 100) #mmol/L
rMM  = Vmax*S/(KM + S)         #mmol/L/s

#######################################################################################
# Illustrate MM rate vs. substrate concentration                                      #
#######################################################################################

plt.figure(figsize = (5, 5))
plt.plot(S, rMM, label = 'Michaelis-Menten Kinetics')
plt.xlabel('Substrate Concentration (mmol/L)')
plt.ylabel('r (mmol/L/s)')
plt.xlim(0, 50)
plt.ylim(0, 1.2)
plt.legend()
plt.show()

The figure shows the relationship between reaction rate and substrate concentration if enzyme kinetics follow a Michaelis-Menten mechanism. We can see the reaction starts off in a positive (first) order regime where rate increases with substrate concentration. Eventually, as substrate concentration becomes large, we can see that the reaction rate no longer responds to further increases in substrate concentration (zero order). The region where rate is zero order in substrate concentration is indicative of what we call “saturation kinetics.” This means that all of the free enzyme is bound to substrate, so none is left to bind additional substrate. As a consequence, adding more substrate does not increase the rate of reaction.

This is easy to see if we go back to the analytical solution for the concentration of the Enzyme Substrate Complex:

\[[ES] = \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2} [S][E]\]

And the free enzyme concentration:

\[[E] = \frac{[E_t]}{1 + \frac{k_1}{k_{-1} + k_2}[S]}\]

If we substitute the second into the first, we find:

\[[ES] = \frac{K_M[S][E_t]}{\left(1 + \frac{1}{K_M}[S]\right)}\]

Factoring out the (1/KM) term before [S] in the denominator, we get a cancellation and are left with the following expression:

\[[ES] = \frac{[S][E_t]}{K_M + [S]}\]

When the substrate concentration is low (\([S] \rightarrow 0\)), then \(K_M >> [S]\), and we find:

\[[ES] = \frac{[E_t]}{K_M}[S]\]

When the substrate concentration is high (\([S] \rightarrow \infty\)), then \(K_M << [S]\), and we find:

\[[ES] = [E_t]\]

Remember: the rate of this reaction is fundamentally given by \(r = k_2[ES]\). At low concentrations of substrate, we can see that adding more substrate increases the concentration of [ES] and so increases the rate of reaction. Once the substrate concentration is high enough, we find ourselves in the position where all of the enzyme is bound to substrate. Adding more substrate does not change the concentration of the enzyme substrate complex in this regime because we have saturated the available enzyme.
